Basics about Microscope

发表于2024-01-27|更新于2024-01-27|BCS
|阅读量:

Basics

0. Side Tichniques and Terms

The Parameters of a Microscope

  • Magnification: the ratio of the size of an image produced by a microscope to the size of the object
  • Resolution: the minimum distance between two objects that can still be distinguished as separate objects
    • spatial
    • temporal
    • angular
    • spectral
  • Numerical Aperture (NA): the ability of an optical system to gather light
    • n: refractive index of the medium between the object and the lens
      $\theta$: half of the angle of the maximum cone of light that can enter the lens

  • Depth of Field (DOF): the distance between the nearest and the farthest objects in focus

    • n: refractive index of the medium between the object and the lens
      $\lambda$: wavelength

  • Field of View (FOV): the area of the object that can be seen with the microscope

  • Artifacts: the unwanted features that appear in images produced by the microscope

    The Detector

Light -> electrons -> voltage -> digital signal?

  1. Analog image sensor: PMT
    • PMT: photomultiplier tube
      • multiplies the current produced by incident light by as much as 100 million times
  2. Digital image sonsor
    • CCD sensors: charge-coupled device
    • CMOS sensors: Active pixel sensor (APS)

Optical sectioning

  • 3D reconstruction of a sample form images taken at different focal planes.
  • A new alternative to thin sectioning
  • z resolution:
    • n: refractive index
    • $\lambda$: wavelength
  • Higher NA, thinner DOV, lower z resolution, higher optical sectioning
  • Improving optical sectioning:
    • Confocal Microscopy
    • Light Sheet Microscopy
    • Two-photon Microscopy (toghether with confocal microscopy)
    • Deconvolution

Image deconvolution

  • The inverse process of convolution, both are used in signal processing and image processing
  • where $f$ is the original image, $g$ is the filter, $h$ is the blurred image (recorded signal)
  • Optical Imaging: $g$ is the point spread function (PSF)
    • usually an approximation of the real PSF, theoretical or experimentally determined
    • When the PSF is unknown: blind deconvolution

Point Spread Function (PSF)

  • linear property of an imaging system:

  • PSF: the impulse response of an imaging system
    • the image of a point source
    • the object is divided into many weighted point sources, the image is the sum of the images of all the point sources

  • Entirely determined by the optical system (microscope)

  • suitable for other fields: astronomy

1. Fluorescence Microscope

Principles

  • Majority: Epofluorescence Microscope
  • High SNR: 入射光大部分不会被反射，Objective接收的主要是激发光
  • resolution <-> NA
  • dichroic mirror: 每次成像只能成像一种波长，多种颜色需要多次成像合成

    Systems

  • Light Source
  • (Light Display Optics): basically direct illumination
  • Light Assembly Optics: collection method of light(lenses, mirrors, filters)
  • Dectction, Amplification, Visualization: CCD, PMT, etc to detect and quantify emitted photons

    Light Sources

    intense, mono-chromatic

Four main types:

  • Mercury-vapor lamps/ Xenon arc lamps
  • Lasers(Confocal microscopy, TIRF microscopy)
  • Supercontinuum sources
  • High-power LEDs

Sample Preparation

（这部分以前看过一些，粗略分个类）

  • Biological fluorescent stains (fluorescent dyes): higher brightness and stability (no maturation time 不用蛋白质表达)
  • Fluorescent proteins: GFP
  • Fluorescent antibodies(Immuno-fluorescence)

    Lamitations

  1. Photobleaching
    • light-chemical reaction damages the fluorophore
    • limit the time of observation
    • minimize illumination intensity, enhance robustness of fluorophore
  2. Phototoxicity
  3. Allows only observation of specific labled structures

    Sub-diffraction techniques

Basic Issue: Diffraction limit

(Super-resolution microscopy)

  • STED microscopy: stimulated emission depletion microscopy
  • Vertico SMI
  • SPDMphymod
  • PALM: photoactivated localization microscopy

    2. Confocal Microscope

    Laser Scanning Confocal Microscopy (LSCM).

  • pinhole: reduce out-of-focus light but also reduce the amount of light that reaches the detector
  • useful to image thick fixed samples, high resolution

    Techniques

  • Laser Scanning: slow and unfit for live imaging, sample fixed
  • Spinning Disk: multiple pinholes, reducing excitation energy, therefore less photobleaching and phototoxicity
  • Programmable Array Microscope (PAM): moving pinholes made of SLM; CCD camera

Variants & Enhancements

  • Improving axial resolution: 4Pi microscopy, confocal theta microscopy, Light sheet microscopy; additional deconvolution
  • Super-resolution: STED microscopy, PALM, STORM, SIM

3. Two-photon Microscope

Nonlinear optics…

  • well suited for imaging scattering live tissue up to 1 mm in depth
  • two-photon excitation: two photons of lower energy are absorbed by a fluorophore, which then emits a single photon of higher energy
  • typically uses near-infrared excitation light (minimize scattering in tissue, reduce phototobleaching and phototoxicity)
  • Much more efficient: even scattered photons can be detected
  • A superior alternative to confocal microscopy for thick tissue imaging
  • Precise localization (like confocal microscopy)
  • Lamitations:
    • need pulse laser: severe photobleaching(square of intensity), much more expensive than continuous wave laser in confocal microscopy

  • Higher order multiphoton excitation: three-photon excitation

4. Light Sheet Microscope


  • faster than point-scanning techniques
  • need the whole sample to be translated and rotated

    5. Light Field Microscope

  • scanning free
  • 3D imaging
  • subsecond(~0.1s), large volumetric imaging(~0.1 to 1 mm^3), high spatial resolution(~1 um)
  • Two steps: light field capture and processing

    Principles: theory of light field

  • Light field: 4D function describing the amount of light traveling in every direction through every point in space
  • Light field camera: capture the light field

  • Intensity:

    $f$ is the distance between the lens and the sensor

    $s,t$ are the coordinates of the microlens array

    $u,v$ are the coordinates of sensors

  • Components of LFM:
    • wide field microscope
    • microlens array
    • CCD or CMOS sensor
  • Spatial resolution: determined by the number of microlenses
  • Angular resolution: determined by the number of pixels behind each microlens

Applications

  • Calcium imaging
  • measuring the neuron dynamic activities
